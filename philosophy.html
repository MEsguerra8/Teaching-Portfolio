Coming Home to

Teaching

"Seeing children learn and grow has always been the most meaningful part of teaching for me."

When I was a kid, I wanted to be many things. First, a doctor like many young girls in my time. But I knew my family couldn t afford medical school, and that dream quietly faded. Later, I dreamed of becoming a stewardess so I could travel while earning a living, until I was told I wasn t tall enough. In high school, my Christian Living Education classes shaped me deeply, and for a time I imagined becoming a missionary going to remote places and sharing my time, talent, and treasure. But that too came with a hard reality: my family needed my support after graduation, and I had no way to fund a life of travel and service.

A few months before graduating high school, I had an epiphany: I would become a teacher. It felt like the closest path to the life I had imagined one where I could still serve others, but with a steady income. Teaching also ran in my family: my paternal grandfather was a principal, my maternal grandmother was a teacher, and two of my aunts were educators. So studying education felt both natural and practical a path I could take without resistance, and one that led straight to earning a living.

Teaching wasn’t my dream at first, but I grew to love the classroom because of the kids.

That's not to say it was easy; my students were adolescents, constantly shifting between wanting independence and still relying on adults for guidance. Still, it was deeply rewarding to see their eyes light up, whether they finally grasped a grammar rule or discovered something unexpected, like how the castration of Cronus led to the birth of Aphrodite.

Those moments became the heart of my teaching.

I knew they would carry what they learned beyond the classroom to build their futures. While my immediate goals were for them to pass my subject, graduate, and find work, my deeper aim was to nurture a lasting love for learning. I wanted them to become lifelong learners—not only of English, but of the kinds of knowledge that lead to a meaningful and fulfilled life.

My Personal Teaching Style

My style has always leaned toward the Communicative Approach. I believe students learn best when they are engaged, heard, and given meaningful opportunities to use the language.

In my class, you'll likely find:

  • Stories, songs, and videos
  • Games and creative activities
  • Group work and presentations
  • Role-plays that bring stories to life

Yes, we study grammar and take tests—because goals matter. But I also believe learning should be fun, meaningful, and memorable. Through activities like role-plays, comics, news reports, or modern adaptations, students often learn without realizing they’re building all four skills.

My goal has always been to help students not just pass the subject, but to become lifelong learners.

My Teaching Techniques

I create a classroom where students feel comfortable, respected, and motivated to participate.

  • Build Relationships: I connect with my students so they feel safe enough to be themselves and take risks.
  • Use Warm Language & Teacher Talk: It lowers their affective filter and makes learning less intimidating.
  • Engage and Empower: Through group work, role-playing, songs, games, and real-life connections.
  • Practice with Purpose: Guided practice in groups. Independent practice through worksheets and homework.
  • Assess and Celebrate Growth: Through creative presentations and tests—so students see how far they've come.
