My Full Teaching Philosophy

Why I Teach

When I was a kid, I wanted to be many things. I wanted to be a doctor, just like many young girls in my time did. I knew though that my family wasn’t rich enough to send me to medical school, so that dream fizzled away. When I got a little older, I wanted to be a stewardess — being a flight attendant and being able to visit many places while earning seemed really enticing, until I was told that I wasn’t tall enough for the job. In high school, my Christian Living Education classes had a huge impact on me and how I wanted to live my life; for a time, I dreamt of living a missionary’s life — being sent to remote places and sharing with people my gifts of time, talent, and treasure. But where was I going to get money to fund my trips and serve people? My parents were counting on me to help out with household expenses once I graduated, so the missionary dream flew right out of the window. A few months before my high school graduation, I had an epiphany: I was going to be a teacher. Teaching was the closest I could get to becoming a missionary — I’d get to do the same things I wanted to, but with a paycheck. Plus, teaching runs in the family. My paternal grandfather was a principal in a local elementary school. My maternal grandmother was a teacher, as well as two of my aunts. Taking up education in college felt like the most natural thing to do. I took up teaching because it was the most convenient course of study, and a straight path to earning a living that my parents wouldn’t have any objections about. Teaching wasn’t my dream, but I ended up loving being in the classroom because of the kids. That’s not to say that teaching was a bed of roses; my students were adolescents, rebels torn between having autonomy and being kids who depended on adults for everything. So it was very rewarding for me to see those kids’ eyes light up — be it because they finally understood how a certain grammar rule worked or because it was cool to know that the castration of Cronus led to the birth of Aphrodite. Seeing that lightbulb moment became my goal in teaching. I knew that my students would use the things they learned in the classroom to better themselves and to go out into the world and reach for their dreams. While my short term goals include having my students pass my subject, graduate, and get jobs, my ultimate long-term goal is to inculcate in kids the love for learning. I want my students to be lifelong learners — not just of English, but of things that are essential to living a fruitful and fulfilled life.

My Personal Teaching Style

I have not been actively teaching in the classroom for over a decade now, but when I was teaching, my style gravitated toward the Communicative Approach. I was teaching Filipino high schoolers, and my students were at a stage when they needed to assert and express themselves more — so droning on about grammar rules and bombarding them with characters and plots from assigned readings just wouldn’t do the trick. They needed activities that would engage them with the topic, be it about language or literature. I tried to vary my activities as much as possible, knowing that each student is different (Cognitive Approach). I understood that the wider I cast my net, the more I’d reach my students. I tried my best to use teacher talk and warm language in every lesson to lower my students’ affective filter. To get their attention, I used stories, songs, and video clips before I started my lessons. I also used games whenever I saw fit. Sure, there was a big chunk of input from me especially during grammar lessons, but I tried to balance this with guided practice through group work (quiz bees, class presentations, jingle-making, among others) and independent practice through worksheets, reporting, or sometimes homework. I remember even making my students re-imagine the circles of hell and putting their own spin on Dante’s Inferno through an illustration, and having them create comics featuring the Olympian Gods. Of course, since the goal of my students was to pass the subject, I also gave tests to measure how much of the material they retained. My favorite activity (and I guess my students’ too) was the role-playing kind. After discussing a literary piece, I would group my students and have them put up presentations about the reading. This was when the classroom would light up with their creative output — the students would act out the story, make ads about it, create a modern adaptation, or even make a news report out of it. These activities did not just make the material fun for the students, but also got them to use English without them noticing that they were actively learning all four skills (reading, writing, listening, and speaking). I’m taking this course because I wish to go back to teaching. But I aim to transition into teaching young learners English as a second language. If I were teaching preschoolers who are just beginning their English language journey, I would need to use different techniques such as reading levelled to learners' knowledge, grammatical items from reading, and vocabulary from the Reading Approach. I think that reading aloud, Q&A and conversation practice from the Direct Approach would also be useful in teaching young learners. I would also use drills (sparingly) from the Audio-Lingual Approach, as well as realia and visuals, which are techniques from the Comprehension Approach. Teaching English need not be limited to one approach or three. I believe that all the concepts I learned (and re-learned) in the previous courses will help me understand and adjust better so that I can help my students achieve their own goals for learning the language.

My Teaching Techniques

When I was still teaching high school, I used a variety of techniques, which of course included teacher talk and warm language. As I have proven with my high school students then, being a terror teacher doesn’t work. Students are more receptive when they don’t have their guard up, so these strategies are very helpful. By using teacher talk, I was able to lower my students’ affective filter; they didn’t feel like they had to “perform” for me, which resulted in them in authentically learning the concepts and skills I taught. My students liked it better when they could move and “do their thing in the classroom,” so I was big on positive reinforcement, group work, role-playing, and other activities that stimulate their creative juices, and allow them to use their past knowledge and incorporate it with new concepts they learned in class. I was trained the old-school way, so I can’t say that I followed the 80-20 rule. My classes weren’t always teacher-led, but I admit that most of the time, when there wasn’t group work or presentations, I was the one doing the talking. But since realizing the wisdom behind the 80-20 rule, I have committed myself to following that moving forward. Sure, there might be some instances where this rule can’t be realistically followed, but I will make sure that my students can have more opportunities to practice English in the classroom. I didn’t do anything special in my classes to motivate my students, but for me the biggest motivation was the relationship that I had with them. Outside the classroom, they could joke with me and talk with me like I was their friend, but they knew that I meant business inside the classroom. Songs, games, personal stories, scaffolding, and speaking their language made introducing lessons easier. For guided practice, putting my students in groups and having them process the concepts taught was effective because they didn’t only check their understanding against that of their classmates, they also got their classmates’ take on the matter. The more insights they shared, the better their understanding of the topic. As for individual practice, I usually gave worksheets and homework. Evaluation and assessment of students came in the form of presentations (role-playing, comic drawing, news casting, ad creation, etc) and paper and pencil tests. The first was for me to check how well they have learned the concepts and translate these concepts on a new form, while the testing was to ensure that they got a grade that I can compute (which served to show if they failed the subject, passed it with flying colors, and everything in between).
