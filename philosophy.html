Coming Home to

Teaching

Seeing children learn and grow has always been the most meaningful part of teaching for me.

When I was a kid, I wanted to be many things.

When I was a kid, I wanted to be many things. I wanted to be a doctor, just like many young girls in my time did. I knew though that my family wasn’t rich enough to send me to medical school, so that dream fizzled away. When I got a little older, I wanted to be a stewardess — being a flight attendant and being able to visit many places while earning seemed really enticing, until I was told that I wasn’t tall enough for the job. In high school, my Christian Living Education classes had a huge impact on me and how I wanted to live my life; for a time, I dreamt of living a missionary’s life — being sent to remote places and sharing with people my gifts of time, talent, and treasure. But where was I going to get money to fund my trips and serve people? My parents were counting on me to help out with household expenses once I graduated, so the missionary dream flew right out of the window.

A few months before my high school graduation, I had an epiphany: I was going to be a teacher. Teaching was the closest I could get to becoming a missionary—I’d get to do the same things I wanted to, but with a paycheck. Plus, teaching runs in the family. My paternal grandfather was a principal in a local elementary school. My maternal grandmother was a teacher, as well as two of my aunts. Taking up education in college felt like the most natural thing to do. I took up teaching because it was the most convenient course of study, and a straight path to earning a living that my parents wouldn’t have any objections about.

“

I didn’t start out dreaming of being a teacher,
but I fell in love with it because of the kids.

That’s not to say that teaching was a bed of roses; my students were adolescents, rebels torn between having autonomy and being kids who depended on adults for everything. So it was very rewarding for me to see those kids’ eyes light up—be it because they finally understood how a certain grammar rule worked or because it was cool to know that the castration of Cronus led to the birth of Aphrodite. Seeing that lightbulb moment became my goal in teaching.

“ Seeing those students to be lifelong learners.

📚 My Personal Teaching Style

My style gravitated toward the Communicative Approach. In my class, you'll likely find:

  • Stories, songs, and videos
  • Games and creative activities
  • Group work and presentations
  • Role-plays that bring stories to life

💡 My Teaching Techniques

I create a supportive and engaging classroom. In my class, I:

  • Build Relationships
  • Use Warm Language
  • Engage and Empower
  • Practice with Purpose
  • Assess and Celebrate Growth
