Coming Home to

Teaching

Seeing children learn and grow has always been the most meaningful part of teaching for me.

When I was a kid, I wanted to be many things. I wanted to be a doctor, just like many young girls in my time did. I knew though that my family wasn’t rich enough to send me to medical school, so that dream fizzled away. When I got a little older, I wanted to be a stewardess — being a flight attendant and being able to visit many places while earning seemed really enticing, until I was told that I wasn’t tall enough for the job. In high school, my Christian Living Education classes had a huge impact on me and how I wanted to live my life; for a time, I dreamt of living a missionary’s life — being sent to remote places and sharing with people my gifts of time, talent, and treasure. But where was I going to get money to fund my trips and serve people? My parents were counting on me to help out with household expenses once I graduated, so the missionary dream flew right out of the window.

I didn’t start out dreaming of being a teacher, but I fell in love with it because of the kids.

It wasn’t always easy. My students were teenagers—rebels torn between wanting independence and still needing guidance. But nothing beat the joy of seeing their eyes light up—whether it was finally understanding a grammar rule or realizing that mythology could be fascinating.

Seeing that lightbulb moment became my goal in teaching.

I taught for six years, and after that, I stepped away from the classroom and worked in different roles.

During that time, I became a mom to two amazing children. Watching them learn, struggle, and gain confidence every day has deepened my appreciation for how children grow at their own pace. It reminded me—quietly but clearly—of what I have always loved most about teaching: being part of a child’s journey of growth and discovery.

Now that my children are becoming more independent and finding their own paths, I feel ready to return to the classroom. In many ways, it feels like coming home.

My Personal Teaching Style

My style has always leaned toward the Communicative Approach. I believe students learn best when they are engaged, heard, and given meaningful opportunities to use the language.

In my class, you'll likely find:

  • Stories, songs, and videos
  • Games and creative activities
  • Group work and presentations
  • Role-plays that bring stories to life

Yes, we study grammar and take tests—because goals matter. But I also believe learning should be fun, meaningful, and memorable. Through activities like role-plays, comics, news reports, or modern adaptations, students often learn without realizing they’re building all four skills.

My goal has always been to help students not just pass the subject, but to become lifelong learners.

My Teaching Techniques

I create a classroom where students feel comfortable, respected, and motivated to participate.

  • Build Relationships: I connect with my students so they feel safe enough to be themselves and take risks.
  • Use Warm Language & Teacher Talk: It lowers their affective filter and makes learning less intimidating.
  • Engage and Empower: Through group work, role-playing, songs, games, and real-life connections.
  • Practice with Purpose: Guided practice in groups. Independent practice through worksheets and homework.
  • Assess and Celebrate Growth: Through creative presentations and tests—so students see how far they've come.
